## Overall Drafting Direction

The booklet should read like a short practical field guide for first-time AI app builders.

The writing should be:

- plainspoken
- calm
- protective
- direct
- beginner-safe
- practical
- lightly instructive
- not academic
- not technical
- not motivational fluff

The reader should feel that the booklet is helping them protect their idea, not slowing them down for no reason.

The writing should avoid long theory. Each section should move quickly from the warning to the practical decision.

---

## Core Manuscript Shape

The draft should follow this emotional and practical movement:

```text
You are excited.
AI makes it easy to act too soon.
A visible demo can trick you.
An idea is not yet a project.
Before the idea becomes real, define the problem.
Then define the first user.
Then define version one.
Then define what is not included yet.
Then look for stop signs.
Then decide: ready, pause, or do not start yet.
If ready, move into Before the Build Starts.
```

## Section 1: Introduction — Why This Booklet Exists

**Section Purpose**
Explain why builders need a decision stage before starting an AI-assisted build.

This section should establish the core danger: AI can turn vague ideas into real project artifacts before the builder has made the necessary decisions.

**Main Points to Cover**
- First-time AI builders often start with excitement.
- AI tools make it easy to turn that excitement into visible output.
- Visible output can create a sense of progress before the project is clear.
- The danger is not having an idea.
- The danger is letting the idea become a project too early.
- This booklet creates a pause between “I have an idea” and “I am building this.”

**Reader Action / Takeaway**
The reader should answer:

```text
What am I about to ask AI to build?
```

The reader should understand that this question is not enough by itself. It is the starting point for the rest of the booklet.

**Example / Metaphor Direction**
Use a construction-style metaphor lightly:
- Do not pour concrete just because you bought tools.
- Do not start framing a house before deciding what kind of building it is supposed to be.

Keep the metaphor simple and practical.

**Transition Into Next Section**
Move from the general need for a pause into why AI makes that pause harder.

**Estimated Length**
2–3 pages after layout

**Boundaries to Avoid**
- Do not shame the reader.
- Do not say AI builders are dangerous or bad.
- Do not discuss specific platforms.
- Do not introduce technical planning.

## Section 2: Why AI Makes Ideas Feel Ready Too Soon

**Section Purpose**
Show how AI generation creates false momentum and premature confidence.

**Main Points to Cover**
- AI can make an unclear idea look real quickly.
- Screens, files, demo flows, or generated copy can feel like proof.
- A demo is not the same as validation.
- Visible progress can create emotional attachment.
- Once the builder sees something working, they may start defending it instead of questioning it.
- The earlier the project becomes real, the harder it is to step back.

**Reader Action / Takeaway**
The reader should answer:

```text
What would make this idea worth building if AI had not already generated 
anything?
```

This prompt helps separate the idea’s value from the excitement of seeing output.

**Example / Metaphor Direction**
Possible metaphor:
- A cardboard movie set can look real from the right angle, but that does not mean the building is ready to live in.
- A quick demo is like a sketch, not a permit.

Use only one metaphor. Do not overdo it.

**Transition Into Next Section**
Move from false readiness into the need to distinguish an idea from a project.

**Estimated Length**
2–3 pages after layout

**Boundaries to Avoid**
- Do not make broad claims about all AI tools.
- Do not imply demos are useless.
- Do not drift into validation methodology.
- Do not create a fear-based tone.

## Section 3: The Difference Between an Idea and a Project

**Section Purpose**
Define the central distinction of the booklet.

An idea becomes a project only when the builder defines purpose, user, scope, and boundaries.

**Main Points to Cover**
- An idea can be exciting without being ready.
- A project creates obligations.
- A project has files, features, design choices, costs, review work, and future decisions.
- An idea says, “This could be something.”
- A project says, “This is what we are building first.”
- The builder needs a clear boundary between imagining and committing.

**Reader Action / Takeaway**
The reader should answer:

```text
Is this still an idea, or have I already started treating it like a project?
```

Follow-up:

```text
What have I already committed to before defining the project clearly?
```

**Example / Metaphor Direction**
Use a simple “gravity” metaphor:
- Once an idea becomes a project, it starts pulling in decisions.
- Files, features, and early choices create gravity.

**Transition Into Next Section**
Once the reader sees that a project requires commitment, the next question is what problem the project is supposed to solve.

**Estimated Length**
2–3 pages after layout

**Boundaries to Avoid**
- Do not become philosophical.
- Do not discuss legal/business formation.
- Do not drift into project management systems.
- Do not design the project yet.

## Section 4: What Problem Does This Actually Solve?

**Section Purpose**
Help the reader define the real problem instead of describing features.

**Main Points to Cover**
- Many builders describe the app by listing features.
- Features are not the same as the problem.
- AI can generate features even when the problem is unclear.
- A clear problem helps the builder judge what belongs in version one.
- The problem should be described in everyday language.
- The problem should be narrow enough to guide decisions.

**Reader Action / Takeaway**
The reader should complete:

```text
This idea helps __________ do, avoid, decide, understand, or manage __________ 
because __________.
```

Alternative simpler version:

```text
The problem is: __________.
```

The section should encourage the reader to rewrite the problem until it sounds simple.

**Example / Metaphor Direction**
Use a practical example without naming a specific platform.

Example direction:
- Weak: “It has profiles, reminders, dashboards, and AI suggestions.”
- Stronger: “It helps a busy group decide what to watch without spending the whole night scrolling.”

Keep examples generic and brief.

**Transition Into Next Section**
Once the problem is clearer, the next question is who has the problem first.

**Estimated Length**
3–4 pages after layout

**Boundaries to Avoid**
- Do not require customer interviews.
- Do not discuss market size.
- Do not discuss competitors.
- Do not use startup validation jargon.
- Do not turn this into a product strategy chapter.

## Section 5: Who Is Version One Actually For?

**Section Purpose**
Help the reader define the first user for version one.

**Main Points to Cover**
- Version one cannot serve everyone.
- “Everyone” creates too many features.
- The first user helps the builder decide what matters now.
- The first user is not the full future audience.
- The first user is the person version one must help clearly.
- A clear first user makes version one easier to protect.

**Reader Action / Takeaway**
The reader should complete:

```text
The first version is for __________, who needs help with __________.
```

Follow-up:

```text
If this first user is the only person version one helps, what does it need to do 
well?
```

**Example / Metaphor Direction**
Use a line like:
- A first version needs a first person to disappoint or help.
- If the first user is “everyone,” the first version has no anchor.

Keep it gentle, not snarky.

**Transition Into Next Section**
Once the reader knows who version one is for, the next question is what version one needs to include.

**Estimated Length**
2–3 pages after layout

**Boundaries to Avoid**
- Do not create personas.
- Do not discuss branding.
- Do not discuss audience campaigns.
- Do not create marketing strategy.
- Do not use segmentation frameworks.

## Section 6: What Counts as Version One?

**Section Purpose**
Help the reader define the smallest useful version of the idea.

**Main Points to Cover**
- Version one should be small enough to understand, review, and control.
- Small does not mean unimportant.
- Version one should solve one clear problem for one clear first user.
- Version one should be judged by whether it performs that job.
- Every added feature increases complexity, cost, review work, and drift.
- A safer version one protects both the builder and the idea.

**Reader Action / Takeaway**
The reader should complete:

```text
The first useful version only needs to let the user __________.
```

Follow-up:

```text
If this one thing worked, would version one be useful enough to learn from?
```

**Example / Metaphor Direction**
Use a “first safe bridge” or “first room” metaphor:
- Version one is not the whole building. It is the first room that proves the structure can stand.
- Version one is not the whole bridge. It is the first safe crossing.

Choose one and keep it concise.

**Transition Into Next Section**
Once version one is defined, the reader must protect it by deciding what is not included yet.

**Estimated Length**
3–4 pages after layout

**Boundaries to Avoid**
- Do not define screens.
- Do not outline user flows.
- Do not create a data model.
- Do not explain architecture.
- Do not write AI prompts.
- Do not replace *Before the Build Starts*.

## Section 7: What Is Explicitly Not Included Yet?

**Section Purpose**
Help the reader create a “not yet” list.

**Main Points to Cover**
- “Not yet” is a protective decision.
- It keeps future ideas from invading version one.
- Every feature added now creates cost and review burden later.
- A “not yet” list lets the builder save ideas without building them immediately.
- Exclusion is not rejection.
- The list should be visible before the builder asks AI to build anything.

**Reader Action / Takeaway**
The reader should complete:

```text
For version one, we are not building __________, __________, or __________ yet.
```

Follow-up:

```text
Which future feature is most likely to sneak back into version one?
```

**Example / Metaphor Direction**
Use a practical “parking lot” metaphor carefully:
- The “not yet” list is not a trash can. It is a parking lot.
- Park the idea so it does not drive the first version.

Avoid overusing the metaphor.

**Transition Into Next Section**
After defining version one and the “not yet” list, the reader needs to identify what would make the project unsafe to start.

**Estimated Length**
2–3 pages after layout

**Boundaries to Avoid**
- Do not make the reader feel less ambitious.
- Do not imply future features are bad.
- Do not create a long backlog system.
- Do not teach product roadmap planning.

## Section 8: What Would Make This Project Too Expensive, Too Vague, or Too Large?

**Section Purpose**
Help the reader identify early stop signs before building begins.

**Main Points to Cover**
- Some ideas need more clarity before becoming projects.
- Some projects are too large for the builder’s current time, budget, or confidence.
- A stop sign is useful information.
- It is better to notice risk before AI starts generating files and features.
- Early stop signs prevent rework, confusion, and cost exposure.

**Reader Action / Takeaway**
The reader should complete:

```text
This project is not ready if __________.
```

The section should include a simple stop-sign checklist.

**Stop-Sign Checklist Direction**
Use plain-language checks such as:
- I cannot explain the problem in one or two sentences.
- I cannot name the first user.
- Version one has more than one main job.
- I cannot say what is not included yet.
- I need features I do not understand to make the first version work.
- I cannot review whether AI built the right thing.
- The idea depends on too many assumptions I have not decided yet.
- The project feels exciting, but I cannot explain what would make it done.

**Example / Metaphor Direction**
Use a “dashboard warning light” metaphor:
- A warning light does not mean the car is worthless.
- It means do not keep driving as if nothing is wrong.

**Transition Into Next Section**
After identifying stop signs, the reader is ready to make a decision: ready, pause, or do not start yet.

**Estimated Length**
3–4 pages after layout

**Boundaries to Avoid**
- Do not create a complex scoring system.
- Do not give legal, financial, medical, or compliance advice.
- Do not exaggerate danger.
- Do not make the reader feel foolish for pausing.

## Section 9: Ready, Pause, or Do Not Start Yet

**Section Purpose**
Give the reader a simple decision checkpoint.

**Main Points to Cover**
- The reader does not need certainty.
- The reader needs enough clarity to move forward with their eyes open.
- There are three valid outcomes:
  - Ready
  - Pause
  - Do Not Start Yet
- “Pause” is not failure.
- “Do Not Start Yet” does not mean the idea is bad.
- The decision should be based on clarity, not excitement.

**Reader Action / Takeaway**
The reader should choose one:

```text
READY:
The idea has a clear problem, first user, version-one boundary, and not-yet 
list.

PAUSE:
The idea is promising, but one or more basic decisions are still unclear.

DO NOT START YET:
The idea is too vague, too large, too expensive, or too dependent on assumptions 
to become a project right now.
```

**Final Readiness Questions**
Include the following:
- What is the idea?
- What problem does it solve?
- Who is version one for?
- What does version one include?
- What is not included yet?
- What would make this project too expensive, vague, or large?
- Am I ready to turn this idea into a project?

**Example / Metaphor Direction**
Use a simple gate metaphor:
- This is the gate between idea and project.
- The gate is not there to block good ideas. It is there to keep unclear projects from running ahead of you.

**Transition Into Next Section**
If the reader chooses ready, the next job is not building yet. The next job is designing the project before the build starts.

**Estimated Length**
3–4 pages after layout

**Boundaries to Avoid**
- Do not make the decision process complicated.
- Do not introduce numerical scoring.
- Do not require outside validation.
- Do not imply that “ready” means “guaranteed to succeed.”

## Section 10: Bridge to the Next Booklet — Before the Build Starts

**Section Purpose**
End the booklet by preparing the reader for the next Don’t Go Broke booklet without replacing it.

**Main Points to Cover**
- If the idea is ready to become a project, the next step is still not “ask AI to build it.”
- The next step is to design the project before the build starts.
- This booklet helped decide what the project is allowed to become.
- *Before the Build Starts* helps define how the project should be designed before AI begins building.
- That next stage covers screens, flows, data, constraints, and handoff details.
- The reader should carry forward the problem, first user, version one, not-yet list, and readiness decision.

**Reader Action / Takeaway**
The reader should leave with:

```text
I know what this project is allowed to become.
Now I need to design it before AI starts building it.
```

**Closing Direction**
The final closing should be direct and memorable.

Suggested closing idea:

```text
You are not ready to build because you are excited.
You are ready to build when the project is clear enough to protect.
```

This exact wording may be used or refined in draft.

**Estimated Length**
1–2 pages after layout

**Boundaries to Avoid**
- Do not teach screens, flows, or data models.
- Do not create a build blueprint.
- Do not provide AI builder prompts.
- Do not summarize the next booklet in too much detail.
- Do not make this booklet feel incomplete.

## Risks to Watch During Drafting

**Risk 1: Too Much Workbook Energy**
The prompts should help the reader decide. They should not turn the booklet into a long worksheet.

**Risk 2: Too Much Startup Language**
Avoid terms like validation, market fit, customer discovery, positioning, TAM, moat, MVP unless they are clearly unnecessary. Prefer plain phrases like “is this clear enough to build?”

**Risk 3: Too Much Technical Planning**
Do not drift into screens, flows, data, constraints, architecture, or handoff. Those belong in *Before the Build Starts*.

**Risk 4: Too Much Fear**
The warning should feel practical. The reader should feel more capable, not scared.

**Risk 5: Too Much Abstraction**
Use concrete, everyday phrasing. The reader should know what to write down by the end of each major section.
