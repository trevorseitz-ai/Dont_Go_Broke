## Review Summary

`03-draft-copy.md` is approved for final-copy preparation.

The draft successfully follows the approved concept, brief, outline, and section plan.

The manuscript presents a short, practical field guide that helps first-time AI app builders decide whether an idea is ready to become a real project before they choose a platform, create a blueprint, open an AI agent, or ask AI to generate code.

The draft preserves the central lesson:

**An AI app idea should not become a real project until the builder has clearly defined the problem, the first user, version one, and what is not included yet.**

The draft does not require major structural revision before final copy.

---

## Required Section Check

The draft includes all required sections from the Publication Brief.

**1. Introduction: Why This Booklet Exists**

**Status:** Present

The draft explains why builders need a decision stage before starting an AI-assisted build. It frames the pause as protection, not hesitation.

**2. Why AI Makes Ideas Feel Ready Too Soon**

**Status:** Present

The draft clearly explains how visible AI-generated output can create false momentum and premature confidence.

**3. The Difference Between an Idea and a Project**

**Status:** Present

The draft establishes the central distinction between an idea and a project, including the obligations created once a project becomes real.

**4. What Problem Does This Actually Solve?**

**Status:** Present

The draft helps the reader define the problem in plain language instead of listing features.

**5. Who Is Version One Actually For?**

**Status:** Present

The draft helps the reader define the first user without drifting into personas, market segmentation, or campaign planning.

**6. What Counts as Version One?**

**Status:** Present

The draft defines version one as the smallest useful first version and emphasizes reviewability, clarity, and control.

**7. What Is Explicitly Not Included Yet?**

**Status:** Present

The draft presents the “not yet” list as a protective decision rather than a lack of ambition.

**8. What Would Make This Project Too Expensive, Too Vague, or Too Large?**

**Status:** Present

The draft includes practical stop signs and keeps the warning tone protective rather than fear-based.

**9. Ready, Pause, or Do Not Start Yet**

**Status:** Present

The draft gives the reader a clear decision checkpoint with three valid outcomes.

**10. Bridge to the Next Booklet: Before the Build Starts**

**Status:** Present

The draft clearly points to *Before the Build Starts* without replacing its content.

## Required Concept Check

The draft includes the required concepts from the brief.

**AI speed can make unclear ideas look more valid than they are**

**Status:** Present

The draft repeatedly explains that AI can turn vague ideas into visible output quickly, which may create premature confidence.

**Visible progress is not the same as a good project decision**

**Status:** Present

The draft states this directly and supports it throughout the early sections.

**A demo is not proof that the idea deserves to become a real product**

**Status:** Present

The draft clearly separates demos, screens, generated features, and visible output from actual validation.

**Before building, the builder must define the job the idea is supposed to do**

**Status:** Present

The problem-definition section directly supports this requirement.

**The first user matters more than the full feature list**

**Status:** Present

The draft emphasizes that version one needs a first user, not everyone.

**Version one should be small enough to understand, review, and control**

**Status:** Present

The version-one section makes this a central requirement.

**Every added feature increases cost, complexity, review burden, and drift**

**Status:** Present

The draft states this clearly in the version-one and “not yet” sections.

**“Not yet” is a protective project decision**

**Status:** Present

The draft strongly preserves this idea.

**The builder needs a boundary between the idea and the project**

**Status:** Present

The idea/project distinction is the structural center of the draft.

**The goal is not to kill ideas, but to prevent premature projects**

**Status:** Present

The draft repeats this framing in several places and keeps the tone constructive.

**Build with your eyes open**

**Status:** Present

The draft explicitly uses this concept in the stop-sign and decision sections.
