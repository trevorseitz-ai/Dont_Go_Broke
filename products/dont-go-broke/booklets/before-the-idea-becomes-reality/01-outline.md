## Purpose of This Outline

This outline defines the structure of the booklet *Before the Idea Becomes Reality* before section planning or drafting begins.

This file does not contain final booklet copy.

This outline establishes:

- the recommended booklet structure
- the purpose of each section
- the reader outcome for each section
- practical prompts or checkpoints to include
- the required bridge into *Before the Build Starts*
- boundaries to preserve during drafting

---

## Core Editorial Spine

The booklet should follow this reader journey:

```text
AI makes ideas feel ready too soon.
↓
An idea is not the same as a project.
↓
The reader defines the real problem.
↓
The reader defines the first user.
↓
The reader defines version one.
↓
The reader defines what is not included yet.
↓
The reader identifies early stop signs.
↓
The reader decides: ready, pause, or do not start yet.
↓
If ready, the reader moves into Before the Build Starts.
```

The booklet should feel like a short practical field guide, not a course, workbook, startup manual, or technical planning document.

### Section 1: Introduction — Why This Booklet Exists

**Required Section From Brief**
Yes

**Purpose**
Introduce the reason this booklet exists: AI builders can now turn vague ideas into visible projects very quickly, but visible progress is not the same as a good project decision.

**Key Points**

- First-time AI builders often feel pressure to start immediately.
- AI tools can create screens, files, features, and assumptions before the project is clear.
- The danger is not excitement.
- The danger is turning an unclear idea into a real project too early.
- This booklet creates a pause before that happens.

**Reader Outcome**
The reader understands that slowing down before building is not hesitation. It is protection.

**Practical Prompt**
“What are you about to ask AI to build?”

**Boundary Notes**

- Do not shame the reader for being excited.
- Do not imply that planning must be heavy or formal.
- Do not start explaining platform choice or build process.

---

### Section 2: Why AI Makes Ideas Feel Ready Too Soon

**Required Section From Brief**
Yes

**Purpose**
Explain how AI generation creates false momentum and premature confidence.

**Key Points**

- AI can make an idea look real before it is clear.
- A generated demo can feel like validation.
- Fast output can create emotional attachment.
- Early files, screens, and features create project gravity.
- The builder may start protecting the thing AI made instead of questioning whether the project is right.

**Reader Outcome**
The reader can recognize that AI-generated motion is not proof that the idea deserves to become a real project.

**Practical Prompt**
“What would make this idea worth building even if AI had not already generated anything?”

**Boundary Notes**

- Avoid fear marketing.
- Avoid claims about specific tools.
- Avoid overexplaining technical systems.

---

### Section 3: The Difference Between an Idea and a Project

**Required Section From Brief**
Yes

**Purpose**
Create the central distinction of the booklet: an idea becomes a project only when purpose, user, scope, and boundaries are defined.

**Key Points**

- An idea can be exciting without being ready.
- A project creates obligations.
- A project has files, scope, decisions, cost, and review burden.
- An idea says, “This could be something.”
- A project says, “This is what we are building first.”
- The builder needs a boundary between the idea and the project.

**Reader Outcome**
The reader understands that moving from idea to project is a decision, not an automatic next step.

**Practical Prompt**
“Is this still an idea, or have you already started treating it like a project?”

**Boundary Notes**

- Do not become philosophical or abstract.
- Keep the distinction practical and grounded.

---

### Section 4: What Problem Does This Actually Solve?

**Required Section From Brief**
Yes

**Purpose**
Help the reader define the real problem instead of describing features.

**Key Points**

- Features are not the same as the problem.
- A vague problem creates a vague project.
- AI can build features without knowing whether they matter.
- The reader should describe the problem in plain language.
- The problem should be specific enough that version one can be judged.

**Reader Outcome**
The reader leaves with a plain-language problem statement.

**Practical Prompt**
“This idea helps _ do / avoid / understand _ because __.”

**Checkpoint**
If the reader can only describe features, the idea is probably not ready to become a project yet.

**Boundary Notes**

- Do not introduce formal customer validation frameworks.
- Do not turn this into market research.
- Do not require interviews, surveys, or business-model analysis.

---

### Section 5: Who Is Version One Actually For?

**Required Section From Brief**
Yes

**Purpose**
Force clarity about the first user.

**Key Points**

- Version one is not for everyone.
- The first user matters more than the full future audience.
- “Anyone who needs this” is usually too vague.
- A clear first user helps reduce features and decisions.
- The first user gives the builder a way to judge whether version one works.

**Reader Outcome**
The reader identifies a specific first user for version one.

**Practical Prompt**
“The first version is for _, who needs help with _.”

**Checkpoint**
If the reader cannot name the first user, the project is not ready for AI-assisted building.

**Boundary Notes**

- Do not drift into audience personas or marketing segmentation.
- Keep this beginner-safe and practical.
- Do not turn the section into a branding or sales exercise.

---

### Section 6: What Counts as Version One?

**Required Section From Brief**
Yes

**Purpose**
Define the smallest useful first version.

**Key Points**

- Version one should be small enough to understand, review, and control.
- A smaller version one is not a smaller dream.
- It is the safest first proof that the project can become real.
- Version one should solve one clear problem for one clear user.
- The more features added now, the more cost, complexity, review burden, and drift the builder accepts.

**Reader Outcome**
The reader defines a smaller and safer version-one boundary.

**Practical Prompt**
“The first useful version only needs to let the user __.”

**Checkpoint**
If version one requires too many features to explain simply, it is probably too large.

**Boundary Notes**

- Do not design screens.
- Do not define user flows.
- Do not specify data models or architecture.
- Those belong in *Before the Build Starts*.

---

### Section 7: What Is Explicitly Not Included Yet?

**Required Section From Brief**
Yes

**Purpose**
Help the reader create a “not yet” list that protects the project from premature expansion.

**Key Points**

- “Not yet” is a protective project decision.
- Every yes creates future cost.
- Future ideas can be preserved without being built now.
- The “not yet” list keeps version one from becoming the whole imagined product.
- Excluding features now does not mean abandoning them forever.

**Reader Outcome**
The reader creates a short list of things that are explicitly not included in version one.

**Practical Prompt**
“For version one, we are not building _, _, or __ yet.”

**Checkpoint**
If everything feels too important to exclude, the idea is not ready to become a controlled project.

**Boundary Notes**

- Keep the tone encouraging.
- Do not make scope control feel like a lack of ambition.

---

### Section 8: What Would Make This Project Too Expensive, Too Vague, or Too Large?

**Required Section From Brief**
Yes

**Purpose**
Help the reader identify early stop signs and risk indicators before building begins.

**Key Points**

- Some ideas need more clarity before they become projects.
- Some version-one plans are too large for the reader’s current time, budget, or confidence.
- Warning signs should be noticed before AI starts building.
- Early stop signs are not failure.
- They help the reader avoid preventable rework, cost, and confusion.

**Reader Outcome**
The reader identifies risks that would make the project unsafe or premature to start.

**Practical Prompt**
“This project is not ready if __.”

**Possible Stop Signs**

- The problem is still vague.
- The first user is unclear.
- Version one has too many jobs.
- The project depends on features the reader does not understand.
- The idea requires too much time, money, or review for the reader’s current situation.
- The reader cannot explain what is not included yet.

**Boundary Notes**

- Do not create a full risk management framework.
- Do not give financial or legal advice.
- Do not exaggerate danger.

---

### Section 9: Ready, Pause, or Do Not Start Yet

**Required Section From Brief**
Yes

**Purpose**
Give the reader a practical decision checkpoint.

**Key Points**

- The reader does not need a perfect plan.
- The reader does need enough clarity to move forward with their eyes open.
- The decision should be one of three states:
  - Ready
  - Pause
  - Do Not Start Yet
- “Pause” is a useful decision, not a failure.
- “Do Not Start Yet” means the idea may need to stay an idea until the basics are clearer.

**Reader Outcome**
The reader can decide whether the idea is ready to become a project.

**Decision Checkpoint**
The idea may be ready if the reader can clearly answer:

- What is the idea?
- What problem does it solve?
- Who is version one for?
- What does version one include?
- What is not included yet?
- What would make this project too expensive, vague, or large?

**Boundary Notes**

- Do not overcomplicate the scoring system.
- Do not create a detailed assessment framework.
- Keep the decision practical and usable.

---

### Section 10: Bridge to the Next Booklet — Before the Build Starts

**Required Section From Brief**
Yes

**Purpose**
Prepare the reader for the next booklet without replacing it.

**Key Points**

- If the idea is ready to become a project, the next step is not immediately asking AI to build.
- The next step is designing the project before the build starts.
- *Before the Build Starts* covers the blueprint stage.
- That next booklet deals with screens, flows, data, constraints, and handoff.
- This booklet’s job ends once the reader has decided what the project is allowed to become.

**Reader Outcome**
The reader understands what comes next in the product ladder.

**Practical Closing Line Direction**
The closing should leave the reader with a simple transition:

“You are not ready to build because you are excited. You are ready to build when the project is clear enough to protect.”

**Boundary Notes**

- Do not teach the blueprint process.
- Do not provide AI prompts.
- Do not drift into implementation planning.

### Recommended Back Matter

Optional back matter may include:

1. One-page readiness checklist
2. One-page “not yet” list
3. Simple next-step note pointing to *Before the Build Starts*

Back matter should remain short.

Avoid turning the booklet into a long workbook.

---

### Recommended Length Guidance

This booklet should remain short and practical.

Recommended target:

```text
20–30 pages after layout
```

The manuscript should be concise enough to feel like a field guide rather than a full manual.

Each section should be useful on its own, but the full booklet should move quickly toward the final readiness decision.

---

### Practical Elements to Include

The booklet should include lightweight reader-facing tools:

- plain-language fill-in prompts
- short checkpoints
- simple warning signs
- ready / pause / do not start decision
- “not yet” list

The booklet should not include:

- long worksheets
- research assignments
- customer interview scripts
- competitive analysis
- business model canvases
- technical design templates
- platform-selection tools
- AI prompt templates

## Required Boundary Checks for Drafting

Before drafting, Publishing Factory must confirm that every planned section passes these checks:

**Platform-Independent Check**
The section does not depend on or recommend any specific AI builder, coding assistant, development platform, or hosting tool.

**Beginner-Safe Check**
The section can be understood by a first-time AI app builder without formal product, startup, or software development knowledge.

**No Startup Validation Drift Check**
The section does not become market research, business planning, customer discovery, or startup strategy.

**No Build-Planning Drift Check**
The section does not teach screens, user flows, data models, implementation architecture, or AI handoff planning.

**No Prompt Engineering Drift Check**
The section does not teach the reader how to prompt AI to build the app.

**No Replacement Check**
The section does not replace *Before the Build Starts*.

## Risks to Watch in Section Planning

**Risk 1: The Problem Section Could Become Market Research**
The problem section should help the reader describe the problem clearly. It should not require formal validation.

**Risk 2: The First User Section Could Become Marketing Strategy**
The first user section should clarify who version one is for. It should not become audience segmentation or campaign planning.

**Risk 3: The Version One Section Could Become Product Design**
The version-one section should define boundaries. It should not design screens, flows, or architecture.

**Risk 4: The Stop Signs Section Could Become Fear-Based**
The stop signs should feel protective and practical, not discouraging.

**Risk 5: The Bridge Could Repeat the Next Booklet**
The bridge should point to *Before the Build Starts*, not summarize or replace it.
