# Purpose

This Publication Concept defines the editorial reason for creating **Before the Build Starts**.

This document is not a Publication Brief.

This document exists to decide whether the booklet should exist, what lesson it should teach, what mistake it should prevent, where it belongs in the Don’t Go Broke product ladder, and what boundaries must be respected before the publication is handed to Publishing Factory.

---

# Publication Thesis

First-time AI builders often move from idea to implementation too quickly.

AI coding tools make it feel possible to start building immediately, but the speed of the tool can hide the fact that the builder has not yet designed the application.

**Before the Build Starts** teaches that a builder should create the blueprint before asking an AI tool or coding agent to build the house.

The booklet exists to help first-time AI builders slow down at the right moment: after they have an idea, but before they start generating code, screens, databases, features, agents, automations, or deployments.

---

# Why This Publication Should Exist

Don’t Go Broke exists to help first-time AI builders avoid expensive, predictable mistakes before they become costly.

Most platform-specific guides warn readers about what can go wrong once they are already inside a tool.

This booklet addresses an earlier mistake.

The reader may not yet have chosen Replit, Vercel, v0, Base44, Google Antigravity, or any other AI building platform. They may still be carrying only an idea, a rough product concept, or a vague app they want to create.

That is the moment when many expensive mistakes begin.

The builder thinks:

> “I know what I want. I just need the AI to build it.”

But the AI cannot protect the builder from an unclear product.

If the app has no blueprint, the builder is likely to burn credits, time, attention, and confidence while repeatedly asking the AI to fix problems that should have been solved before implementation began.

This booklet should exist because the Don’t Go Broke series needs a pre-platform guide that teaches builders how to prepare before the meter starts running.

# Core Editorial Question

What should a first-time AI builder know before turning an idea into a build request?

---

# Core Lesson

Do not ask an AI tool to build the application until you have designed the application clearly enough to know what the tool is supposed to build.

---

# Reader Problem

The reader has an idea for an app, tool, website, automation, or digital product.

They may be excited because AI tools make building feel accessible.

They may also be inexperienced, budget constrained, and unsure how much planning is enough before they begin.

The reader is likely to underestimate the difference between:

* having an idea
* describing a feature
* designing an application
* instructing an AI tool
* reviewing what the AI tool built
* knowing whether the output matches the original intention

Without a design layer, the reader may ask an AI platform to build too soon and then spend the rest of the project reacting to avoidable confusion.

---

# The Mistake This Publication Prevents

This booklet prevents the mistake of treating an AI coding tool as a substitute for application design.

The specific mistake is:

> Starting the build before creating the blueprint.

This mistake creates downstream problems:

* The AI builds the wrong thing.
* The builder cannot tell whether the output is correct.
* Feature requests drift because the product was never defined.
* Screens appear before user flows are understood.
* Data structures are created before the information model is clear.
* The agent keeps making changes without a stable target.
* The builder spends credits fixing confusion instead of building from clarity.
* The builder relies on the AI to fill planning gaps, leading to unexpected feature bloat.
* Hidden costs (tokens, data transfer, SaaS add-ons) accumulate before the builder notices.
* The AI proposes unrequested third-party integrations that inflate the budget.
* The project becomes harder to review, revise, price, explain, or finish.

The publication should teach that many AI-building failures are not coding failures.

They are design failures that were allowed to become coding problems.

---

# What This Publication Teaches

This booklet teaches the reader to create a basic application blueprint before building.

The blueprint does not need to be formal, technical, or professional.

It does need to be clear enough to answer practical questions before the AI starts generating implementation.

The booklet should teach the reader to define:

* what the application is for
* who it serves
* what problem it solves
* what measurable success looks like (success metrics)
* what the first version must do
* what the first version must not do
* what explicit constraints must be given to the AI (e.g., no extra screens or services)
* what the cost ceiling is for tokens, APIs, and SaaS add-ons
* what user flows matter
* what screens are required
* what information the app stores
* what features are essential
* what features can wait
* what constraints matter
* what the AI tool should build first
* what the human must review before allowing the next step

The emphasis is not “plan forever.”

The emphasis is:

> Design enough that building becomes safer.

---

# What Changes In The Reader

Before reading this booklet, the reader believes the next step after having an idea is to start prompting a builder tool.

After reading this booklet, the reader understands that the next step is to create a blueprint.

The intended transformation is:

## Before

The reader thinks:

* “I should start building before I lose momentum.”
* “The AI will figure out the structure.”
* “I can clean it up later.”
* “I just need the first version to exist.”
* “Planning will slow me down.”

## After

The reader thinks:

* “I need to know what I am asking the AI to build.”
* “A small blueprint will save money later.”
* “The first version should be constrained.”
* “Screens, user flows, data, and features need to agree before implementation.”
* “I should review the plan before the agent starts.”
* “The fastest safe build starts with a clear target.”

The reader should leave with more confidence, not more fear.

They should feel capable of taking a practical next step before opening an AI builder.

# Product Ladder Fit

**Before the Build Starts** belongs near the beginning of the Don’t Go Broke product ladder.

It sits after idea-level awareness and before platform-specific execution guides.

The product ladder position is:

1. **Before the Idea Becomes Reality**
   Helps the reader decide whether the idea is clear enough to become a project.

2. **Before the Build Starts**
   Helps the reader create the application blueprint before code is generated.

3. **Before the Agent Starts**
   Helps the reader prepare instructions, constraints, review points, and guardrails before an AI agent begins making changes.

4. **Platform-Specific Survival Guides**
   Helps the reader avoid tool-specific mistakes, costs, limits, and workflow traps.

5. **Don’t Go Broke Starter Bundle**
   Collects proven beginner lessons into a stronger entry product.

6. **Longer Books and Manuals**
   Provide deeper guidance for AI builders who want a fuller operating system for building without wasting money.

This booklet is not the first idea filter.

It is the bridge between idea and implementation.

---

# Product Role

This booklet should function as a pre-build safety guide.

It should be useful before the reader chooses a platform.

It can also be useful after a failed first build, when the reader realizes they need to step back and define the application before continuing.

Its role is to make platform-specific guides more effective by teaching the reader not to arrive at the platform with only a vague idea.

# Editorial Scope

This booklet should cover the minimum useful planning layer before implementation.

It should focus on practical clarity, not professional product management.

The booklet may include:

* the house blueprint analogy
* the difference between idea, feature, app, and build
* the risk of starting with prompts instead of design
* a beginner-safe application blueprint
* first-version constraints
* user flow basics
* screen mapping basics
* data model basics
* feature map basics
* “must build now” versus “can wait”
* decision points before selecting a platform
* review checkpoints before coding or agent execution
* warning signs that the reader is not ready to build yet

---

# Editorial Boundaries

This publication is not a technical architecture manual.

It should not become a coding guide, platform review, product management textbook, startup strategy book, UX design course, or software engineering curriculum.

It should not require the reader to know advanced terms.

It should not tell the reader to create professional-grade specifications before doing anything.

It should not create so much planning burden that the reader feels unable to start.

The goal is practical preparation, not perfection.

---

# What This Publication Is Not

This publication is not:

* a coding course
* a product management course
* a UX design textbook
* a startup validation framework
* a market research guide
* a business planning guide
* a platform comparison guide
* a technical architecture document
* a database design manual
* a prompt engineering guide
* an AI agent operations manual
* a deployment checklist
* a replacement for the platform-specific survival guides

It should not promise:

* a successful app
* a profitable product
* a perfect design
* a complete technical specification
* zero build mistakes
* zero cost
* a guaranteed launch

---

# Reader Level

The reader is a first-time or early AI builder.

They may be:

* a solo founder
* a student
* a side-project builder
* a hobby developer
* a non-technical founder
* a small business owner trying to build an internal tool
* a beginner using AI coding tools for the first time

They may have enough confidence to describe an idea but not enough experience to know what design decisions are missing.

The booklet should assume intelligence but limited experience.

It should never shame the reader for not knowing how software is planned.

---

# Reader Constraints

The booklet must respect that the reader may have limited:

* budget
* time
* technical skill
* product experience
* attention
* confidence
* tolerance for rework
* ability to recover from unexpected costs

Advice should be short, practical, and usable.

The reader should be able to act on the booklet without buying software, hiring a consultant, or becoming a product manager.

# Core Analogy

The central analogy is:

> Do not start building the house before you have the blueprint.

The analogy should be used carefully.

The point is not that the reader needs architectural drawings.

The point is that even a simple build needs a plan for what is being built, how the parts connect, and how the builder will know whether the result matches the intention.

For AI builders, the blueprint is a simple application design.

---

# Recommended Publication Promise

A short, practical guide to designing the app before asking AI to build it.

---

# Recommended Reader Outcome

By the end of the booklet, the reader should be able to create a simple pre-build checklist or blueprint that includes:

* the app’s purpose
* the target user
* the core job the app performs
* the project success metrics
* the first-version boundary
* the main user flow
* the required screens
* the basic data model
* the feature map
* the known constraints
* the cost ceilings and hidden-cost awareness
* the explicit AI constraints (preventing over-generation)
* the build sequence
* the review checkpoints

The reader should know whether they are ready to ask an AI tool to start building.

# Key Concepts To Preserve

The future Publication Brief should preserve these concepts:

* Build with your eyes open.

* AI speed does not replace product clarity.
* Shift planning to the builder, not the AI.
* The agent cannot read your mind.

* Hidden costs and SaaS add-ons start accumulating on day one.

* First-version constraints protect the budget.
* Explicit AI constraints prevent feature bloat and over-generation.

* The goal is not to plan forever; the goal is to avoid preventable rework.
