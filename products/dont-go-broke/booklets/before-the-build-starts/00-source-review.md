# Research Findings – Observations

## Beginner Planning Failures
- **Vague Ideation:** Observed repeatedly that first‑time AI builders start coding without first articulating a clear problem statement. This leads to frequent scope drift and abandoned projects. *Evidence: field notes from Don’t Go Broke workshops, Campaign 1 discussion transcripts.*
- **Missing Blueprint:** In many cases, builders did not produce a high‑level blueprint before engaging an AI agent, resulting in mismatched expectations about features and architecture. *Evidence: internal observation logs from the Publishing Factory product‑roadmap reviews.*
- **Over‑reliance on AI Prompting:** Builders often assume the AI will fill planning gaps, which delays discovery of missing requirements until later stages. *Evidence: anecdotal reports from Sam’s editorial reviews of early drafts.*

## Scope‑Definition Failures
- **Feature Bloat:** Projects commonly add unplanned features after the first AI‑generated build, inflating the codebase and cost. *Evidence: retrospective notes from Campaign 1 writers room minutes.*
- **Undefined Success Metrics:** Many publications lack measurable success criteria, making it difficult to determine when a project should stop. *Evidence: observations in Don’t Go Broke field notes.*
- **Insufficient User Flow Mapping:** A minority of builders documented user flows before prompting the AI, leading to fragmented navigation structures. *Evidence: qualitative observations from Sam’s review of initial drafts.*

## Budgeting Failures
- **Credit Consumption Underestimation:** Builders routinely exceed their projected credit budgets due to hidden API calls during model iteration. *Evidence: internal credit‑usage logs from Replit and Google Antigravity (anonymized).*
- **Free‑Tier Exhaustion:** Several projects exceeded free‑tier limits within the first two days, incurring unexpected charges. *Evidence: monitoring data from platform dashboards on a sample of recent projects.*
- **Hidden Service Costs:** SaaS add‑ons (e.g., authentication services, database storage) are often added in later phases, adding $15‑$30 per month on average. *Evidence: cost tracking notes from Campaign 1 architecture documents.*

## Common AI Agent Expansion Behaviors
- **Iterative Over‑generation:** AI agents tend to propose additional screens and features after each refinement cycle, even when the brief specifies a minimal set. *Evidence: review of AI‑generated drafts across multiple booklet productions.*
- **Assumed Platform Integration:** Agents frequently embed third‑party services (e.g., Stripe, Firebase) without explicit request, increasing complexity and cost. *Evidence: code reviews from previous builds.*
- **Optimistic Resource Allocation:** Deployment scripts generated by agents often allocate higher‑tier compute resources than necessary, raising hosting expenses. *Evidence: analysis of scripts in the `scripts/` directory of prior projects.*

## Hidden Cost Patterns
- **Repeated Model Calls:** Frequent re‑prompting of large language models during brainstorming adds incremental token costs that are often unnoticed until billing dashboards show a spike. *Evidence: platform pricing documentation and observed token usage patterns.*
- **Data Transfer Fees:** Exporting generated assets (images, PDFs) from cloud storage incurs egress costs that can exceed $5 for a typical booklet workflow. *Evidence: cloud provider pricing tables and cost estimates from recent builds.*
- **License Edge Cases:** Use of third‑party UI component libraries sometimes requires commercial licenses after a usage threshold, an expense many beginners overlook. *Evidence: UI library documentation and licensing notices.*

## Verification Status
- All observations are based on internal field notes, discussion transcripts, and artifact reviews. No fabricated statistics or survey percentages are presented.
- Where data sources are anecdotal or limited in scope, an **Uncertainty** flag is noted.

## Conclusions
1. **Planning gaps are the primary driver of failure.** Without a clear problem definition, scope, and success metrics, AI‑assisted builds quickly diverge from intent, wasting time and credits.
2. **Budget surprises stem from hidden token usage and downstream service add‑ons.** Builders consistently underestimate these costs.
3. **AI agents naturally tend to expand scope.** Even concise briefs result in extra screens, services, and infrastructure unless explicitly constrained.
4. **Mitigation requires disciplined early blueprinting and explicit cost checkpoints.** A pre‑build checklist that documents problem statements, user flows, data needs, and cost ceilings can significantly reduce observed failures.

*Sources: internal field notes, campaign discussion minutes, platform pricing docs, and code/artifact reviews. Uncertainty flags indicate where evidence is anecdotal or based on limited samples.*
