# Publication Concept

## Publication Information

**Working Title:** Before You Trust the First Draft
**Publication Type:** Booklet
**Series:** Don’t Go Broke
**Version:** v1.0
**Status:** Approved

## Purpose of This Concept

This Publication Concept defines why *Before You Trust the First Draft* should exist before any outline, draft, or production work begins.

It is the editorial discovery artifact for this booklet.

Its job is to define the booklet’s purpose, lesson, mistake prevented, reader transformation, place in the Don’t Go Broke product ladder, and boundaries.

## Why This Publication Should Exist

First-time AI builders often mistake generated output for trustworthy progress.

They ask an AI builder to create the first version of a product, and the tool returns something that looks real enough to continue.

There are screens. There is logic. There are flows. There is copy. There are features. There may even be a working demo.

That creates a dangerous feeling:

“It looks real, so maybe it is right.”

But the first AI-generated draft is often the moment when the builder is most vulnerable to false confidence.

The project can look more complete than it is. The tool can make assumptions the builder never approved. Important decisions can be hidden inside code, data structure, user flow, permissions, defaults, edge cases, pricing logic, or feature behavior. A polished interface can hide weak thinking. A working demo can hide fragile logic. A confident answer can hide a bad decision.

This booklet should exist to interrupt that moment.

It teaches the reader that the first draft is not the point where trust should begin. It is the point where review should begin.

The first draft is not proof that the product is correct. It is the first thing that must be questioned.

## Core Reader Problem

The reader has already moved past idea-stage and build-preparation work.

They now have a first AI-generated version, or something close to it.

Their risk is no longer only vague planning. Their risk is trusting what AI produced before they understand what it changed, assumed, included, excluded, or got wrong.

They may feel:

- relieved that something finally exists
- tempted to keep building on top of it
- impressed by how complete it looks
- unable to tell what is correct and what is guesswork
- afraid of slowing down the momentum
- unsure what to review first
- unqualified to judge the output
- tempted to show other people before they understand what they are showing

They need a practical review framework before confidence hardens around a draft that has not earned trust.

## Core Lesson

The first AI-generated draft is not something to trust automatically.

It is something to review, question, verify, and constrain before continuing, sharing, or building on top of it.

## Mistake This Publication Prevents

This booklet prevents the reader from treating the first AI-generated version as trustworthy progress before it has been reviewed.

The specific mistake is:

- accepting the first draft as “good enough” because it looks complete, works in places, or feels like momentum.

That mistake can lead to:

- building on top of hidden assumptions
- keeping features the project did not need
- missing broken logic beneath polished output
- carrying forward bad structure
- trusting code or flows the builder cannot explain
- showing others a draft the builder does not understand
- increasing scope before validating correctness
- confusing generated activity with reviewed progress
- making later fixes more expensive

## Reader Transformation

### Before Reading

The reader has a first draft, prototype, or generated version of the project.

They may feel a mix of relief, urgency, confusion, pride, and pressure to keep going.

They may believe the main work now is to refine, expand, polish, or launch what AI already produced.

They may not yet have a clear way to answer:

- What did AI actually decide for me?
- What in this draft is real progress and what is guesswork?
- What needs to be reviewed before I continue?
- What should I verify before showing this to someone else?
- What is safe to keep, and what should be cut, rewritten, or rebuilt?
- What does “good enough to continue” actually mean?

### After Reading

The reader understands that a first draft is not the finish of the first build stage.

It is the beginning of review.

They should leave with:

- a calmer, more skeptical relationship to AI-generated output
- a practical way to inspect the first draft
- a clearer sense of what must be verified before trust
- better language for distinguishing “looks complete” from “is sound”
- more willingness to stop, cut, or redo weak output
- more confidence in reviewing what AI changed
- a stronger ability to continue only from a reviewed foundation

The reader should feel less dazzled, less rushed, and more capable of judging the draft in front of them.

## Product Ladder Role

*Before You Trust the First Draft* is the third booklet in the non-platform Don’t Go Broke sequence.

It comes after:

1. Before the Idea Becomes Reality
2. Before the Build Starts

Its role is to protect the reader after AI has produced the first real output but before the builder treats that output as trustworthy progress.

This booklet sits at the first-review stage.

Its job is not to help the reader decide whether the idea should exist. Its job is not to help the reader design the project before the build. Its job is to help the reader review what AI built before confidence, continuation, or public exposure turns the first draft into a false foundation.

It leads naturally toward later work that includes iteration, refinement, launch, and post-launch responsibility.

## Required Editorial Angle

This booklet should feel like a practical warning for the moment when a builder sees the first AI-generated version and feels tempted to believe it.

The tone should be:

- calm
- direct
- protective
- skeptical in a useful way
- beginner-safe
- plainspoken
- confidence-correcting, not confidence-destroying

It should not shame the reader for being impressed by the draft.

It should not present AI as dishonest magic.

It should help the reader understand that review is part of building, not a sign that they failed.

## Required Concepts

The booklet must include the following concepts:

- The first draft is not proof; it is material for review.
- AI can generate convincing output around assumptions the builder did not approve.
- A polished surface can hide weak logic, bad scope, missing constraints, or fragile behavior.
- What works in one path may still fail in others.
- The builder must know what changed, what was assumed, and what still needs verification.
- “It seems to work” is not the same as “I understand and trust it.”
- Review comes before continuation, sharing, polish, or launch.
- Some output should be kept.
- Some output should be cut.
- Some output should be rewritten.
- Some output should be rebuilt from a clearer instruction.
- If the builder cannot explain what the draft is doing, the builder should not trust it yet.
- Build with your eyes open.

## Required Questions the Booklet Should Help the Reader Answer

The booklet should help the reader answer:

1. What did AI actually produce?
2. What did AI decide or assume without me noticing?
3. What in this draft is clear, and what is still unclear?
4. What must I review before I continue building?
5. What should I verify before showing this to anyone else?
6. What parts of the draft are safe to keep?
7. What parts should be cut, simplified, rewritten, or rebuilt?
8. What would make this draft unsafe to trust yet?
9. What does “reviewed enough to continue” actually mean?

## What This Publication Is Not

This booklet is not:

- a software QA manual
- a professional testing handbook
- a code-review book for engineers
- a debugging course
- a prompt-engineering manual
- a platform-specific guide
- an anti-AI argument
- a launch guide
- a replacement for product planning
- a replacement for post-launch monitoring

It should not become:

- a deep technical testing framework
- a comprehensive security manual
- a formal product-review workflow for teams
- a lecture about perfectionism
- a generic “AI makes mistakes” essay

It should not teach the reader to distrust everything forever.

It should teach the reader that trust must be earned through review.

## Platform Position

This booklet is platform-independent.

It applies whether the reader used:

- Replit
- Vercel
- Base44
- v0
- Antigravity
- Cursor
- Claude
- ChatGPT
- Lovable
- Bolt
- any other AI builder or agentic development tool

The focus is not on the tool. The focus is on the builder’s responsibility after the first draft appears.

## Audience

The primary audience is first-time AI app builders who have already generated an initial draft and now need help reviewing it before treating it as real progress.

Examples include:

- solo founders
- side-project builders
- students
- hobby developers
- non-technical founders
- small business owners experimenting with AI tools
- builders who can see that “something exists” but are not sure whether it is sound

The reader is not assumed to be technical.

The reader is assumed to be able to notice what feels off, but not always to know how to review it systematically.

They need practical review guidance in plain language.

## Emotional Promise

The reader should feel:

- less intimidated by the first draft
- less likely to mistake polish for correctness
- more willing to question what AI produced
- more capable of reviewing before continuing
- more comfortable cutting or redoing weak output
- more confident that slowing down now protects the project later

## Editorial Boundary

This booklet should protect the reader from false confidence in the first draft.

It should not drift backward into idea definition or build-planning.

It should not drift forward into launch operations.

The central editorial question is:

How should a first-time AI builder review the first generated draft before trusting it?

Everything in the booklet should serve that question.
