# Publication Brief

## Publication Information

**Title:** Before You Trust the First Draft
**Publication Type:** Booklet
**Series:** Don’t Go Broke
**Version:** v1.0
**Status:** Approved for Publishing Factory handoff

## Purpose

Create a short, practical booklet for first-time AI builders that teaches them how to review the first AI generated version of a product before trusting it, continuing it, showing it to others, or building on top of it.

The booklet should help the reader identify hidden assumptions, weak logic, false completeness, and unsafe confidence before the first draft becomes the foundation for later work.

## Audience

This publication is for first-time AI app builders who have already moved from idea to build and now have a first generated version, draft, prototype, or demo in front of them.

Primary readers include:

- solo founders
- side-project builders
- students
- hobby developers
- non-technical founders
- small business owners experimenting with AI tools

Audience characteristics:

- beginner to early-intermediate experience
- limited budget
- limited time
- limited technical confidence
- likely to feel pressure to preserve momentum
- likely to be impressed by visible progress
- likely to be unsure what to review first
- at risk of trusting output they cannot fully explain

The booklet must be understandable and useful to someone who can see that AI produced “something real,” but who does not yet know how to judge whether that something is trustworthy enough to continue.

## Core Lesson

The first AI-generated draft is not something to trust automatically; it is something to review, question, verify, and constrain before continuing, sharing, or building on top of it.

## Required Concepts

The publication must include the following concepts:

- The first draft is not proof that the project is correct.
- Generated output can look more complete than it really is.
- AI often fills gaps with normal-sounding assumptions the builder did not explicitly approve.
- A polished interface can hide weak logic, missing constraints, or fragile behavior.
- “It runs” is not the same as “I understand what it is doing.”
- What works in one path may fail in others.
- Review must happen before more building, polish, sharing, or launch decisions.
- The builder needs to inspect what AI changed, added, assumed, or connected.
- Some parts of a draft may be worth keeping.
- Some parts may need to be cut, rewritten, simplified, or rebuilt.
- If the builder cannot explain the draft clearly, the draft has not yet earned trust.
- Review is part of building, not a delay from building.
- Build with your eyes open.

## Required Sections

Publishing Factory may improve structure and wording, but it may not remove these sections without escalation.

Required sections:

1. **Introduction: Why This Booklet Exists**
   Explain why the first draft is a dangerous confidence point for beginner AI builders.

2. **Why the First Draft Feels More Trustworthy Than It Is**
   Explain how visible output, polish, and momentum create false confidence.

3. **The Difference Between Generated Output and Reviewed Progress**
   Clarify that something existing is not the same as something being understood or approved.

4. **What Did AI Actually Decide for You?**
   Help the reader notice hidden assumptions, defaults, structure, and guessed behavior.

5. **What Should Be Reviewed First?**
   Give the reader a practical review order that does not assume deep technical skill.

6. **What Looks Fine but Might Not Be Fine?**
   Teach the reader to question polished surfaces, smooth demos, incomplete paths, and invisible weaknesses.

7. **What Is Safe to Keep, and What Should Be Cut or Rebuilt?**
   Help the reader evaluate whether parts of the draft should remain, be simplified, be rewritten, or be rebuilt from clearer instructions.

8. **Before You Show It to Someone Else**
   Explain what should be understood or verified before the reader shares the draft publicly, with collaborators, or with early users.

9. **Continue, Rework, or Stop**
   Give the reader a practical way to decide whether the draft is trustworthy enough to continue, needs revision, or should not be built on yet.

10. **Bridge to the Next Stage**
    End by clarifying that reviewed progress is the foundation for later iteration, launch, and post-launch responsibility, including the concerns covered in *After Launch, the Meter Still Runs*.

## Constraints

Hard requirements:

- platform-independent
- beginner-safe
- plain language
- short, practical, and direct
- no hype
- no guru tone
- no fear marketing
- no shame-based language
- no unsupported claims
- no platform-specific workflows
- no engineering-jargon overload
- no code-review handbook framing
- no QA-manual framing
- no security-audit framing
- no launch-guide framing
- no replacement for build planning or post-launch operations

Editorial constraints:

- The booklet must help the reader review the first draft before trusting it.
- The booklet must not drift backward into idea validation or blueprint planning.
- The booklet must not drift forward into launch operations or long-term maintenance systems.
- The booklet must not assume the reader is an engineer.
- The booklet must not become a highly technical testing framework.
- The booklet must distinguish between:
  - generated output
  - reviewed progress
  - trustworthy continuation

Tone constraints:

- calm
- protective
- practical
- clear
- plainspoken
- skeptical in a useful way
- beginner-respectful
- confidence-correcting without becoming anti-AI or anti-builder

## Reader Transformation Target

Before reading, the reader is likely to feel some combination of relief, pride, momentum, uncertainty, and pressure to keep building on top of the first generated version.

After reading, the reader should have:

- a clearer understanding of what the first draft is and is not
- a practical review mindset
- a more disciplined way to inspect what AI generated
- better judgment about what to keep, question, cut, rewrite, or rebuild
- more caution before sharing the draft with others
- more confidence in slowing down before continuing
- a more trustworthy foundation for later iteration

## Mistake This Publication Must Prevent

This publication must prevent the reader from treating the first AI-generated version as trustworthy progress before it has been reviewed.

The booklet should interrupt the pattern of:

AI generates draft → draft looks real → builder assumes it is sound → builder keeps building on top of it → hidden assumptions and weak structure become harder and more expensive to fix

## Publishing Outcome Requirement

Publishing Factory should produce a booklet that feels like a practical field guide for reviewing the first AI generated draft before trusting it.

The final publication should leave the reader feeling more grounded, more skeptical in a healthy way, and more capable of saying:

- this part is clear
- this part is assumed
- this part needs verification
- this part should be cut
- this part should be rebuilt
- this draft is not ready to trust yet
- or this draft has been reviewed enough to continue
