# Booklet Standard v1.0

## Purpose

A booklet teaches one lesson and prevents one mistake.

It is a practical field guide.

It is not a worksheet.

It is not a workbook.

It is not a software specification.

---

# Definition

A booklet exists between:

* short pamphlets
* full manuals

A booklet provides enough depth to teach a complete lesson while remaining focused on a single topic.

---

# Target Length

Designed PDF:

20–30 pages

Preferred Target:

24 pages

Acceptable Range:

22–32 pages

Approximate Word Count:

5,000–8,000 words

---

# Reader Outcome

Every booklet must produce:

* one lesson learned
* one mistake avoided
* one practical outcome

---

# Section Count

**6–8 major sections.** This governs drafting new booklets. Retrofit conformance passes on existing booklets are governed by `booklet-payhip-format-conformance-standard-v2_1.md`, not this rule.

A section earns major-section status only if it gives the reader one or more of: a new lesson, a new decision, a new behavior, a new risk to recognize, or a new way to stay in control.

---

# Required Components

Every booklet should include:

## Opening Field Note

Introduce the lesson.

Introduce the mistake.

Create relevance.

---

## Practical Explanation

Explain:

* why the mistake happens
* why it matters
* what to do differently

---

## Examples

Examples should be:

* practical
* realistic
* beginner-friendly

---

## Warnings

Highlight:

* risks
* costs
* tradeoffs

---

## Checklists

Checklists support the lesson.

Checklists are not the lesson.

---

## Closing Note

Reinforce the lesson.

Explain the next logical step.

---

# Litmus Test

Remove:

* checklists
* tables
* worksheets
* templates

If the publication is no longer useful, it is not a booklet.

The lesson must stand on its own.

---

# Not Allowed

A booklet should not become:

* a workbook
* a project specification
* a requirements document
* a software design document
* a collection of checklists
* a platform review
* a course lesson

---

# Tone

A booklet should feel like:

A practical field guide written by someone trying to save another builder from making an expensive mistake.
